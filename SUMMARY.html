Systems assignment · Rust · UDP

A compact transport for a flaky network.

The source emits a 160-byte audio frame every 20 ms, but the path may drop, delay, reorder, or duplicate UDP packets. This solution uses almost the entire permitted bandwidth for causal forward-error correction while keeping the sender and receiver small, bounded, and deadline-driven.

The problem, in plain language

Loss

A missing frame is permanent unless it is resent or reconstructed from redundant information.

Jitter

Even a correct packet is a miss when its random network delay pushes it past the fixed playout deadline.

A hard budget

All bytes entering the relay must stay at or below twice the 160-byte raw stream, including recovery traffic.

End-to-end architecture

Harness source

Produces sequence + 160-byte payload exactly every 20 ms on UDP 47010.

→
Rust sender

Sends the data immediately, then a causal XOR parity equation when the byte budget allows.

→
Hostile relay

Drops, delays, reorders, and duplicates packets before delivering them on UDP 47002.

Wire packet

Two-byte header + 160-byte payload. No handshake, feedback, framework, or side channel.

→
Rust receiver

Unwraps sequence numbers, deduplicates, reconstructs missing frames, and forwards immediately.

→
Harness player

Accepts the first correct copy received by each frame's fixed deadline on UDP 47020.

Wire format and recovery

FieldBits / bytesMeaning
Packet kind1 bit0 is systematic data; 1 is parity.
Sequence15 bitsFrame number modulo 32,768, stored in network byte order and unwrapped at the receiver.
Payload160 bytesData is frame i; parity is a causal XOR equation ending at frame i.

parity(i) = XOR(payload(j)), for j = i - width + 1 ... i

Every data frame is sent immediately. The equation width comes from DELAY_MS: width two at 45 ms, width three at 85 ms, and a conservative maximum of five at longer deadlines. This spends at most half the deadline waiting for future equations while improving tolerance of short correlated loss without tuning for profile A or B.

Whenever an equation has exactly one unknown frame, XOR removes every known member and recovers the missing payload. Recovery then revisits equations containing that frame, so the bounded peeling decoder can unlock a short burst even when parity and data arrive in arbitrary order.

The receiver forwards recovered frames immediately rather than waiting for an internal playout timer. The harness already owns the steady playout clock, and earlier delivery can only improve the deadline result.

Why the bandwidth stays valid

Each relay packet is 162 bytes. In a 30-second run there are 1,500 data packets and 1,462 parity packets:

(1,500 + 1,462) × 162 / (1,500 × 160) = 1.99935×

The sender earns 158 redundancy bytes after each 162-byte data packet and spends 162 credits per parity packet, with at most one parity per input frame. This byte-credit invariant replaces a profile-specific packet schedule; a unit test checks every stream length from 1 through 100,000 frames and proves it never crosses 2.0×. No feedback bytes are emitted.

Memory and latency behavior

Bounded ring

Capacity is derived once from the deadline and FEC span, rounds to a power of two, and is capped at 2,048 entries.

Fixed buffers

Packet buffers and sender history are fixed arrays; receiver slots and its worklist are allocated once before the receive loop.

Bounded work

Ordinary packets examine at most five short equations. A recovery cascade is bounded by the live ring and never grows its worklist.

At the recommended 85 ms delay, the receiver uses only 16 slots (about 5.4 KiB including equation payloads and worklist storage). Packets older than the live window are rejected before they can overwrite a newer slot.

Why causal FEC instead of ARQ

An automatic-repeat request must cross the impaired feedback path, and its reply must cross the impaired media path. At low playout delays, that round trip often finishes after the frame's deadline and consumes bytes even when it cannot help. Causal FEC travels with the live stream and can reconstruct a missing frame as soon as an equation containing only that unknown arrives.

    Every measured run was valid; exact random outcomes remain deterministic for an unchanged packet sequence.

Known limits

  • An equation with two or more unresolved frames cannot be solved until other received equations reduce it to one unknown; sufficiently long correlated erasures therefore remain unrecoverable.
  • A network spike beyond the selected playout delay is unrecoverably late even if its bytes eventually arrive.
  • The 15-bit sequence unwrapping assumes reordering stays below half the modulo range: 16,384 frames, or about 327 seconds.
  • The assignment fixes one stream and fixed ports; production multi-stream scaling would run isolated sessions with distinct endpoints or a connection identifier.
